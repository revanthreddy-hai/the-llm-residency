The Map of the Territory

An LLM is one pipeline: data, pretraining, post-training, evals, inference, operations. None of it is magic, and by the end of this program you will have built every stage yourself. Today you train your first one.

≈ 9 min read Last verified 2026-07-21 Textbook register
Skip test

Answer these three and you can jump straight to the lab:

  1. What does a language model literally output at each step?
  2. Name the two training phases between “pile of web text” and “helpful assistant.”
  3. Why is generating token #500 usually cheaper to compute but slower to serve than token #1?

If any of those made you squint, you’re in the right place. If #3 made you squint, good: that one takes most working ML engineers a while, and it’s Week 13’s whole story.

You have used a large language model. You may have shipped features on top of one. And if you’re honest, somewhere between “it autocompletes text” and “it wrote my SQL migration,” your mental model has a gap labeled ???, or worse, labeled magic.

Here is the claim this entire program stands on: there is no magic anywhere in the pipeline. A large language model is one system with six stages, each of which is ordinary engineering: ambitious, sometimes expensive, occasionally maddening, but ordinary. Data goes in one end. A running product comes out the other. Every stage can be built, measured, broken, and fixed by a person like you.

Over the next six months you will build all six stages with your own hands: you’ll train a transformer from scratch and keep the loss curves to prove it, fine-tune it into an assistant, evaluate it without fooling yourself, compress it, serve it behind an API with a latency target, and keep it alive under load. Roughly ten hours a week. No ML background assumed; if you can program, the on-ramp covers the rest.

This first post is the map. Everything after it is territory.

The one-sentence version

A language model is a function that takes a sequence of tokens and outputs a probability for every possible next token.

That’s the whole product. GPT-5, Claude, Llama, the medical voice agent that schedules your colonoscopy prep call. At the core, each one answers exactly one question, over and over: given everything so far, how likely is each possible next piece? Pick a token from that distribution, append it, ask again. Every chatbot conversation you’ve ever had was generated one probability distribution at a time.

Hold onto how underwhelming that sounds. The gap between that sentence and “it debugged my Kubernetes manifest” is not magic; it’s six stages of engineering, and closing that gap is what the next 26 weeks are for.

The six stages

01Data

Someone has to decide what a model reads, and “the internet” is not an answer; it’s a problem statement. Web crawls arrive as a swamp: boilerplate, duplicates, spam, gibberish, things you legally and ethically must not train on. The real work is filtering, deduplicating, and mixing: how much code, how much prose, how much of which languages. Data work is the least glamorous stage and moves model quality more than almost anything downstream. In Week 7 you’ll run a small pipeline and measure exactly how much dirty data hurts.

02Pretraining

The famous part. Take a transformer (a particular arrangement of matrix multiplications you’ll assemble by hand in Weeks 4–5), show it trillions of tokens, and for each one ask it to predict the next. Grade it, nudge millions of dials in the right direction, repeat until the swamp of text has been compressed into something that completes sentences eerily well. It is also where the money goes: frontier runs are hundred-million-dollar exercises in not wasting GPU time. In Weeks 1–8 you’ll do it at a scale that fits on one GPU, which is enough to hit every real failure mode: loss spikes, learning-rate cliffs, the works.

03Post-training

A pretrained model is a completion engine, not an assistant. Ask it “What’s the capital of France?” and it may reply “What’s the capital of Germany? What’s the capital of Italy?”, because quiz lists look like that on the internet. Post-training teaches it to be useful: supervised fine-tuning on example conversations, then preference optimization so it favors answers humans actually want. Same machinery as pretraining, radically less data, much more judgment. In Weeks 9–12 you’ll turn your own base model into something you can talk to.

04Evals

How do you know any of it worked? Benchmarks, and this deserves the emphasis: evals are the easiest place in the whole pipeline to fool yourself. Test questions leak into training data. Scores swing run to run. Models learn the format of a benchmark instead of the skill. Serious teams treat evaluation as an engineering discipline In Week 8, then again in Week 20, you’ll build an eval harness, run the same benchmark ten times, and look hard at the spread before you ever trust a single number again.

05Inference

I give inference six full weeks because it is the part I most want to understand deeply. Training happens once; inference happens every time any user sends any message, forever. It has its own deep engineering: why generating text is bottlenecked on memory bandwidth rather than raw compute, the KV cache (the data structure behind most of an LLM API’s serving cost), batching strategies, quantization, speculative decoding, and what an H100 actually does for its $30,000. Weeks 13–17 cover all of it, and if you end up specializing where the industry is currently most starved for engineers, it will probably be here.

06Operations

Models are software in production: dashboards, latency targets, load spikes, incidents at 2 a.m. A serving fleet can be up and still be failing: cache pressure quietly evicting context, a quantized deployment silently 3% dumber than yesterday’s. Running models is a reliability discipline, and these notes treat it as one. From Week 18 on, you’ll deliberately break your own serving stack and drill the recovery.

Where the jobs are (and where we go deepest)

Roughly three roles live on this pipeline. Training engineers live in stages 1–3 (data, distributed training, post-training recipes). Inference engineers live in 5–6 (serving systems, GPU economics, latency). Applied LLM engineers build products on top of everything (retrieval, agents, evals for their domain). Teams hire for all three; the middle one is consistently the hardest to fill, because it needs systems engineering and ML fluency in the same head.

This program covers the entire pipeline; you can’t reason about serving a model you don’t understand, so the foundations (Weeks 1–8) come first. But it allocates its depth budget deliberately: six of the twenty-six weeks (13 through 18) live entirely on inference and operations, the slice other courses compress into a single lecture, with inference threads running from Week 6 onward. By Week 16 you will have served your own fine-tuned model under a stated latency target and produced a cost-per-million-tokens report a finance team could audit.

Build one right now

Thirty minutes, laptop CPU, no GPU, no ML background. We’re training the smallest language model that is honestly called one: it reads 32,033 human first names and learns which letter follows which. Then it invents new names.

Open the lab in Colab → Runs in your browser, nothing to install, or copy the code below.

Python 
import torch

# 32k names, one per line; dataset in the lab repo (or karpathy/makemore)
words = open("names.txt").read().splitlines()

# our "tokenizer": one token per character, plus '.' to mark start/end
chars = sorted(set("".join(words)))          # 26 letters
stoi = {s: i + 1 for i, s in enumerate(chars)}
stoi["."] = 0
itos = {i: s for s, i in stoi.items()}

# the entire model: a 27x27 table of next-character counts
N = torch.zeros((27, 27), dtype=torch.int32)          # [V, V] vocab x vocab
for w in words:
    cs = ["."] + list(w) + ["."]
    for c1, c2 in zip(cs, cs[1:]):
        N[stoi[c1], stoi[c2]] += 1

# counts -> probabilities: P[i] answers "given character i, what comes next?"
P = (N + 1).float()                                    # +1: never say "impossible"
P /= P.sum(1, keepdim=True)                            # rows sum to 1.0

# generation: the same sampling loop every LLM uses, behind a much weaker model
g = torch.Generator().manual_seed(42)
for _ in range(5):
    out, ix = [], 0
    while True:
        ix = torch.multinomial(P[ix], num_samples=1, generator=g).item()
        if ix == 0:
            break
        out.append(itos[ix])
    print("".join(out))

Run it (guided version with tests: lab repo → week00/). On my Mac the whole thing, counting through evaluation, takes about a second. With seed 42 the first names out are anugeenvi, s, mabidushan, stan, silaylelaremah: one plausible name, a fourteen-letter ramble, and a name that is just the letter s. All of these outcomes matter:

  • It works. The table looked at real names and now produces name-shaped strings it has never seen. It learned the statistics of a tiny universe. And the sampling loop is the same one every LLM runs: model the probability of what comes next, then sample from it. What differs is the model behind the distribution: a 27-by-27 table here, a transformer with billions of parameters in ChatGPT. You have now trained and sampled from a language model. The sentence at the top of this post should feel less abstract already.
  • It’s terrible. It only remembers one character of context, so a “name” can be a single letter or a fourteen-letter ramble: each step looked locally plausible and the whole is garbage. Fixing that (looking further back, and learning which parts of the past matter) is, in a very real sense, the entire rest of this course. Next week we give it a brain instead of a lookup table. In Week 4 we meet attention, the idea modern LLMs are built on.
