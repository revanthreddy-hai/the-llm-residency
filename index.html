The territory

Six parts of the LLM lifecycle

The rhythm

What I publish each week

Each week ships with something you can run, break, and keep.

Read · ~15 min

The flagship essay

One concept, first principles to production. Every tensor shape-annotated, every claim cited, a skip test up top for readers in a hurry.

Build · ~2–4 h

The lab

Runnable self-checks, with pytest where it adds value. Labs run on Colab or one consumer GPU.

Keep

The reference card

One page, zero narrative: the KV-cache sizing formula, the LoRA decision card, the eval-pitfalls checklist. The thing you bookmark.

Follow

The resident’s log

I’m going through the residency as I write. You get the diary: failures, dead ends, and hours included.

The route

The six-month plan

Each phase ends with a project and a short technical report.

The on-ramp

WEEK 0

The map of the territory, tensors and gradients without tears, your toolchain, and a model you train on day one.

Foundations

WEEKS 1–8

Tokenization, attention, the transformer, training dynamics, data, evals. Ends with a GPT you trained from scratch, plus a written training report.

Adaptation

WEEKS 9–12

SFT, LoRA, preference optimization, distillation. Your base model becomes an assistant you can talk to, with the eval regressions to prove nothing broke.

The inference core

WEEKS 13–18

KV cache, batching, quantization, speculative decoding, GPU cost math, multi-GPU serving, incident drills. Ends with your model served under a stated SLO, and the cost-per-million-tokens report.

Capstone

WEEKS 19–26

Safety guardrails, a benchmark worth trusting, then a scoped capstone built in public, through demo day and a final self-audit against all seventeen competencies.

The curriculum

Every week, mapped

Tap any week for the core idea and its lab. Weeks marked interactive ship a standalone explorable; milestone weeks end with a portfolio artifact. Comfortable programming assumed; everything else is on the syllabus. Plan on about ten hours a week. This is the current plan, not a fixed syllabus; I expect it to change as I work through it.

Phase 1Foundations

WEEKS 1–8
W01A Language Model, End to End

A language model predicts the next token; cross-entropy measures how surprised it was.

LAB · Count-based bigram → neural bigram → MLP, with held-out evaluation from day one.

W02How Text Becomes Numbersinteractive

Many LLM quirks trace back to tokenization: arithmetic, spelling, multilingual cost.

LAB · Build BPE from scratch and train it on a real corpus. Ships with the Tokenizer Playground.

W03Embeddings: The Geometry of Meaning

Embeddings turn tokens into vectors where direction carries meaning.

LAB · Train embeddings; probe analogies and, more instructively, their failure modes.

W04Attention From First Principlesinteractive

Attention is a differentiable lookup: Q asks, K matches, V delivers.

LAB · One causal head from scratch; visualize its weights on real text. Ships with the Attention Visualizer.

W05Assembling the Transformer

The residual stream is a shared bus; every block reads it, modifies it, writes it back.

LAB · Head → block → GPT-2-small shape; RoPE vs learned positions; count the parameters by hand.

W06Training It For Real

Loss curves are dashboards; most training failures have recognizable signatures.

LAB · Train on TinyStories; find four planted bugs from the curves alone; GPU memory accounting: estimate first, then measure.

W07Data Is the Actual Model

Filtering, deduplication, and dataset mixture can materially change model quality.

LAB · Build a mini pipeline; measure exactly what dirty data does to your Week-6 model.

W08Evaluation: How To Not Fool Yourselfmilestone

Benchmark items can contaminate training data, and scores depend on the dataset, prompt, sampling, and judge.

LAB · Build the eval harness used throughout the residency; create held-out data, repeat stochastic measurements, and report uncertainty. MILESTONE · “I trained a GPT”: a training report with curves, samples, evals, and cost.

Phase 2Adaptation & the Inference Core

WEEKS 9–16
W09Base Model → Assistant: SFT

An assistant is a persona trained on top of a base model; a wrong chat template breaks it quietly.

LAB · SFT an open 0.5–3B model on a public instruct set; get loss masking right.

W10LoRA, Derived

Fine-tuning lives in a low-rank subspace; one rented GPU is enough to matter.

LAB · LoRA vs full fine-tune: quality, VRAM, cost, with regressions caught by your Week-8 harness.

W11Preference Optimization

RLHF is a multi-stage pipeline; DPO gives most of the benefit with far less machinery.

LAB · DPO on public preference data; before/after evals, plus a sycophancy check.

W12Distillation: Why Products Shrink Models

A big model can teach a small one; latency-bound products live on this.

LAB · Distill your SFT model into a smaller one; measure the gap honestly.

W13Inference I: The KV Cacheinteractive

During decoding, memory bandwidth limits throughput more than arithmetic does.

LAB · Derive the KV-cache sizing formula; verify it against measurement within 5%; find the OOM cliff. Ships with the KV-Cache Visualizer.

W14Inference II: Batching & PagedAttention

Throughput comes from batching; PagedAttention manages KV memory the way an OS manages virtual memory.

LAB · vLLM vs a naive generation loop: TTFT, ITL, and throughput under real load.

W15Inference III: Quantization & Speculative Decoding

Compare quantization and speculative decoding by quality, latency, memory, and cost.

LAB · Quantize weights and KV (int8 / int4 / FP8); add a draft model; measure everything.

W16Inference IV: GPUs and Moneyinteractive milestone

Roofline thinking: know whether you’re compute-bound or memory-bound before you optimize.

LAB · GPU anatomy, MFU, dollars-per-million-tokens. MILESTONE · your fine-tuned model, served under a stated SLO, with the cost / latency / quality report. Ships with the Roofline & Cost Explorer.

The outcome

Seventeen things you’ll be able to do

Each one is observable, and each has a lab behind it.

Foundations5
  • Train a transformer LM from scratch to a target loss
  • Diagnose training failures from curves and logs
  • Build a data pipeline and quantify its effect
  • Build and train a tokenizer; explain its consequences
  • Build an eval harness; quantify variance and contamination
Adaptation3
  • SFT + LoRA a model with regression discipline
  • Preference-optimize (DPO) and measure the side effects
  • Distill along a quality-per-dollar curve
Inference · the spine7
  • Derive KV-cache and memory math from first principles
  • Reason about prefill, decode, and batching
  • Quantize and speculative-decode with measured tradeoffs
  • Do GPU cost accounting: roofline, MFU, $/1M tokens
  • Serve under an SLO with load tests and rollback
  • Scale serving across GPUs
  • Operate: observability, SLO burn, incident drills
Integration2
  • Ship safety guardrails under a stated risk budget
  • Scope, execute, and communicate an audit-ready capstone

The standard

How each post is checked

I will inevitably get some things wrong. Each post links its sources, records when it was last checked, and publishes corrections openly.

01

Expert review

I try to get each essay reviewed by people already working in this field, from inside the residency or outside it.

02

Code with recorded checks

Each published lab includes visible self-checks and records the environment used to run it.

03

Primary sources only

Technical claims link to papers or official documentation.

04

Corrections in public

Errors get fixed in a public corrections log. Corrections are dated and remain visible.
